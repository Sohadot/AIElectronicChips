Governance · Evidence, claims, and interpretation

Methodology

Credible compute sovereignty analysis begins by separating evidence, interpretation, confidence, and unknowns.

The methodology applies the project's source and claim policies and the Compute Sovereignty Standard to every future record, diagnostic output, and commissioned brief.

Interpretation standardCompute sovereignty is interpreted through layered dependency exposure, not reduced to a single score.
01

Layered interpretation

Findings are read by sovereignty-stack layer and exposure type before cross-layer synthesis. No layer or collection of findings assigns sovereignty.

02

Source hierarchy

Primary, dated, and attributable evidence is preferred: laws and official notices, formal company disclosures, technical standards, patents, peer-reviewed research, and official institutional publications.

03

Claim classes

Material statements are classified as verified fact, attributed statement, estimate, analysis, or unknown. Analysis must remain visibly distinct from the evidence used to derive it.

04

Confidence and insufficient evidence

High, Medium, Low, and Insufficient describe evidence quality and completeness, not favorable capability or rhetorical certainty. Insufficient evidence produces no operational finding.

05

Freshness and changing conditions

Findings carry an as-of date. Changing policy, capacity, ownership, access, and operating conditions require review before reuse.

06

No sovereignty score

The standard prohibits a single score because layers, exposures, confidence, unknowns, and evidence quality cannot responsibly be collapsed into one number.

07

Corrections and conflict

Credible conflicting sources are disclosed rather than averaged away. Material errors require a visible correction describing what changed, when, and why.
